Skills and Reusable Workflows

Introduction to Skills as structured, reusable work instructions — from content preparation to practical application in daily work.

30 min. learning time 5 quiz questions Includes video

1. What are Claude Skills?

A Skill is a permanently defined, reusable work instruction. Unlike a Project, a Skill is portable and can be used in any chat.

PropertyProjectsSkills
PortabilityIsolated — only within the projectUsable in any chat
CombinabilityFixed contextFlexibly combinable
SystematicsManual briefing each timeDefined, repeatable processes
Core idea

Instead of telling the AI what to do every time, you build a collection of clearly defined processes — a personal toolkit that grows with your needs.

2. Knowledge Acquisition with NotebookLM

Before creating a Skill, you need solid knowledge of the topic:

  • Deep Research: NotebookLM searches over 60 sources and compiles the results.
  • Structure knowledge: Transform raw research material into a clear, usable system.
  • Feed into the Skill: The structured knowledge becomes the foundation of the Skill instruction.

3. Configuring the Skill in Claude

In Skill Creator mode, Claude guides you through the setup interactively and asks follow-up questions:

  • What is the goal of this Skill?
  • Who is the target audience?
  • What output format should it produce?
  • In what language should it work?

4. Using the Skill in Daily Work

  • Select via the plus icon in the chat — no long prompt needed.
  • Low prompt effort thanks to anchored expert knowledge and defined processes.
  • Update the Skill as your requirements evolve — it improves over time.

5. Scalability

Skills are not limited to a single use case. The same approach works for:

  • Email funnels and client communication templates
  • Content strategies and editorial planning
  • Financial analyses and reporting workflows
  • Research briefings and company profiles
Practical exercise

Apply what you've learned

Identify your first Skill candidate and learn when a Skill makes more sense than a single prompt.

Exercise 1

Identify your first Skill

The best Skills solve tasks you do repeatedly. Identify the right candidate.

  • Think of 3 tasks you do with Claude regularly (e.g. drafting emails, summarising documents, researching companies).
  • For each task: how often do you do it per week? How long does it take?
  • Choose the task with the highest frequency and time investment — that is your first Skill candidate.
Goal: A Skill that saves you 30 minutes per week saves 26 hours per year.
Exercise 2

Skill or standard prompt?

Not every task justifies its own Skill. Learn to choose the right level of abstraction.

  • Assess these three tasks: (a) A one-off summary of an article, (b) Weekly creation of meeting minutes in the exact same format, (c) Occasional translation of an email into English.
  • Decide for each: is a one-off prompt sufficient, or is a saved Skill more sensible?
  • Justify your decision using these criteria: frequency, consistency requirement, complexity of the instruction.
Example prompt:

I'll give you 3 tasks. For each, decide whether a one-off prompt is enough or a saved Skill would be more sensible. Justify your reasoning using frequency, consistency requirement and complexity. Format: table with columns Task, Recommendation, Justification.

Goal: Choose the right level of abstraction — not everything needs a Skill, but recurring processes benefit enormously.
Sample solution
TaskRecommendationJustification
(a) One-off summary of an articleStandard promptLow frequency, no consistency requirement — a single prompt is entirely sufficient
(b) Weekly meeting minutes in the same formatSkillHigh frequency + fixed format = classic Skill candidate; define once, use permanently
(c) Occasional translation of an emailStandard promptLow frequency, variable context — a Skill would barely save time

Rule of thumb: A Skill pays off from roughly 3–5 repetitions per month, provided the desired format stays the same each time.
